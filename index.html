Pawn: The soul of chess. Moves one square forward, but captures diagonally. On its very first move, it has the option to advance two squares. If it survives the journey to the final rank, it can promote into any other piece, usually a Queen.
Knight: A tactical specialist that moves in an 'L' shape (two squares one way, one square at a 90-degree angle). It is the only piece capable of jumping over other units, making it lethal in closed positions.
Bishop: An elegant long-range attacker that travels any number of squares diagonally. Because it is confined to the colour it starts on, light-squared and dark-squared bishops must work in tandem to control the board.
Rook: A heavy artillery piece that moves horizontally or vertically. It is most effective when controlling open files or paired with another rook on the seventh rank. It also participates in the special 'Castling' move.
Queen: The ultimate weapon. She possesses the combined movement of both the Rook and the Bishop, allowing her to dominate the board in any straight or diagonal direction with unmatched mobility.
King: The heart of the game. He moves one square in any direction. While not powerful offensively, his safety is the ultimate priority; if he is trapped and cannot escape an attack, the game is lost.

Fundamental Rules
Capturing: To capture, move your piece onto the square occupied by an opponent's piece. The captured piece is removed from the board. Unlike some games, capturing is not mandatory in chess.
Castling: A defensive move where the King moves two squares toward a Rook, and the Rook jumps over the King. This can only be done if neither piece has moved and no squares in between are under attack.
Endings
Checkmate: The ultimate goal. This occurs when the King is in check and there is no legal way to move the King, block the attack, or capture the attacking piece. The game ends in a win for the attacker.
Stalemate: A draw that happens when the player whose turn it is has no legal moves, but their King is NOT in check. This is often used as a defensive 'swindle' to save a lost game.
Resignation: A formal concession where a player admits defeat before checkmate occurs. This is considered good sportsmanship when a position is mathematically and strategically hopeless.
Timeout: In timed games, if a player's clock reaches zero, they lose immediately, provided the opponent has enough material to technically deliver a checkmate.
