Pawn The fundamental soul of chess structure. It advances one square forward, yet executes captures diagonally. Upon its initial move, it may leap two squares. Reaching the final rank permits the player to promote it to any high-value piece of their choosing.
Knight An exceptional tactical unit that navigates in a distinctive 'L' pattern—two squares in one cardinal direction followed by a single square perpendicular. It possesses the unique capability to vault over obstructing pieces, making it lethal in cluttered board states.
Bishop A specialized long-range operative restricted to diagonal trajectories. Each Bishop is confined to squares of its starting colour, requiring coordinated movement with its counterpart to control the entire board. It is most effective in open positions with clear lines of sight.
Rook A formidable heavy piece moving in straight horizontal or vertical lines. Its primary functions include controlling open files and securing the King through the castling maneuver. It becomes increasingly dominant during the endgame phase when fewer pieces obstruct its path.
Queen The most versatile and powerful asset on the battlefield. Combining the movement capabilities of the Rook and Bishop, she can slide any distance in any straight or diagonal direction. Her presence dictates the tempo and creates immense pressure on the opposing King.
King The pivotal figure of the game whose safety is paramount. He moves one square in any direction. While offensively limited, his protection determines the outcome; should he be trapped without escape, the game concludes immediately, regardless of any remaining material advantage.
Positive Indicators
⭐Best Move (Star) This designation highlights the absolute strongest continuation identified by the neural engine. Playing this move optimizes your mathematical win probability and represents the highest level of technical accuracy possible in the current game state.
✔️Good Move (Tick) Indicates a solid, fundamentally sound choice that maintains the evaluation of the position. While it may not be the engine's primary preference, it adheres to chess principles and prevents the opponent from gaining any significant counterplay.
Negative Indicators
!Inaccuracy (Exclamation) Marks a sub-optimal move that slightly worsens your standing. It suggests a missed opportunity to apply pressure or a minor positional concession that allows the opponent to improve their pieces more easily than they should.
!!Mistake (Double Exclamation) A significant error that tangibly reduces your advantage or increases the opponent's lead. This usually results from an overlooked tactical sequence or a serious misunderstanding of the immediate requirements of the board position.
💀Blunder (Death Emoji) A catastrophic failure that leads to immediate material loss or a forced checkmate sequence. This move represents a total collapse of strategy and typically shifts the game evaluation into a completely winning state for the opponent.
Exotic Classifications
?Unclear (Question Mark) Used to denote moves that complicate the position in ways the engine cannot immediately resolve. It signifies a high-variance choice that creates a chaotic environment where both players have chances to succeed or fail.
??Brilliant (Double Question) Although visually identical to a blunder in some notation systems, here it highlights a sacrifice that appears as an error but leads to a forced winning advantage upon deeper calculation and precise follow-up.
📖Book Move (Manual) Indicates that the move belongs to the established library of opening theory. This confirms the player is following paths analyzed by grandmasters for centuries to achieve a balanced and playable middle-game position.
Fundamental Mechanics
Capturing Methodology To capture an opponent's unit, you must move your piece onto the square it currently occupies. The displaced piece is permanently removed from the field. You cannot capture your own units or occupy a square already held by your own color.
Ending Conditions
Checkmate (Absolute Victory) The definitive conclusion of a match. This occurs when a King is under direct threat of capture and possesses no legal moves to relocate, block the line of attack, or eliminate the attacking piece entirely.
Stalemate (Technical Draw) A unique situation where the player whose turn it is has no legal moves remaining, yet their King is not currently in check. The game ends in a draw, often occurring in endgames with limited material.
Resignation (Concession) A formal acknowledgement of defeat. A player may choose to resign at any moment if they determine the position is strategically hopeless, thereby awarding the victory to their opponent out of professional courtesy and respect.
Timeout (Temporal Loss) In timed competitive play, if a player's clock expires before they complete the required moves, they lose the game immediately. This emphasizes the importance of time management as a critical component of professional chess mastery.
