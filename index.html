♕ QUEEN (Royal) The Queen is the **most versatile asset**, capable of moving any number of squares straight or diagonally. She combines the firepower of the Rook and Bishop, acting as the primary **offensive powerhouse**.
Example: Sweeping across the board from a1 to h8 to deliver a finishing blow.
| Importance: RED-CRITICAL
♖ ROOK (Heavy) Rooks move horizontally or vertically. They are high-durability units best used for **linear suppression** and controlling open files. Their ability to **castle** provides critical King safety.
Example: Occupying the 7th rank to restrict the opponent's King movement.
| Importance: ORANGE-SIGNIFICANT
♗ BISHOP (Heavy) Bishops are limited to diagonal movement on their starting color tiles. They are excellent for **long-range pinning** and sniping high-value targets from a distance.
Example: Pinning a Knight to the enemy Queen from the opposite corner.
| Importance: ORANGE-SIGNIFICANT
♘ KNIGHT (Minor) Knights move in an 'L' shape and are the only units that can **leap over other pieces**. They are lethal in **closed positions** where other assets are blocked by pawn structures.
Example: Jumping over a pawn wall to "Fork" the King and Queen simultaneously.
| Importance: YELLOW-MODERATE
♙ PAWN (Infantry) Pawns move forward but capture diagonally. While expandable, their **pawn structure** defines the tactical terrain. They can be promoted to any major piece upon reaching the final rank.
Example: Sacrificing a pawn to open a line for a more powerful Rook attack.
| Importance: GREEN-EXPANDABLE

★ BEST The move calculated by the engine as the **highest numerical value**. It is the most consistent and optimal path forward in the current state.
Example: Trading pieces when you are ahead in material to simplify the win.
📖 BOOK A move that follows **established chess theory**. These are opening sequences that have been simulated and proven effective over centuries of play.
Example: Playing the 'e4' pawn opening to control the board centre.
? MISTAKE A sub-optimal move that results in a **significant loss of advantage**. While not immediately terminal, it allows the opponent a strategic opening to counter-attack.
Example: Moving a piece to a square where it can be trapped by a pawn.
?? BLUNDER A **catastrophic error** that leads to the loss of a major piece or immediate checkmate. A blunder usually shifts the win probability entirely to the opponent.
